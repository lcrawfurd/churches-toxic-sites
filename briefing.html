It's not just schools — thousands of churches sit next to toxic sites

Follow-up to the CGD working paper "Schools in the Shadow of Toxic Sites." Draft, 8 June 2026.

Our recent paper showed that more than 250,000 schools in developing countries sit within 5 km of a documented contaminated site. A natural question is whether schools are special, or whether this is just where people — and the institutions that serve them — happen to be. So we looked at another kind of building that communities cluster around: the church.

The answer is that places of worship are exposed too. Using OpenStreetMap data on roughly 220,000 Christian churches across 40 countries, we find that 18,199 of them — about one in twelve — sit within 5 km of a documented contaminated site, and 2,615 are within 1 km. Lead is by far the most common pollutant nearby, present at sites next to more than 9,000 of these churches.

The pattern looks just like the one for schools. Industry, housing, and the buildings where people gather all concentrate in the same crowded, under-regulated urban areas. A church is not near a toxic site because anyone chose to put it there; it is near a toxic site for the same reason a school or a home is.

Where the churches are

Five countries account for most of the affected churches within 5 km: the Philippines (3,079), Brazil (2,144), Indonesia (1,707), Kenya (1,500), and Ghana (1,430). The closest cases — within 1 km — are led by the Philippines, Kenya, Ghana, Brazil, and Tanzania. Of the churches we can classify by denomination, the largest identifiable groups near sites are Catholic (about 4,100 within 5 km) and Anglican (about 150), with most of the rest unlabelled in the data.

Faith institutions run schools, too

This matters beyond the church building itself, because faith organisations educate millions of children near these same sites. In the school-census data behind the main paper, 10,890 Catholic or Anglican schools sit within 5 km of a documented contaminated site, and 2,003 within 1 km. The Catholic and Anglican communions are among the largest non-state education providers in the developing world, and many of their schools share a wall, or a neighbourhood, with the kind of industrial sites we mapped.

Why faith communities could help

Schools cannot fix industrial pollution. Neither can churches. But both are trusted local institutions with reach that governments often lack, and the history of lead campaigns suggests this is not a small thing. In Flint, Michigan, faith leaders and teachers were among the first to press the case that something was wrong with the water.

Three modest roles stand out. First, awareness: a parish or a diocese can tell families near a site what lead exposure does and how to reduce it — hand-washing, keeping toddlers off contaminated soil, testing children's blood lead. Second, pressure: faith networks can ask local authorities for soil and water testing around their buildings and schools, which is cheap to do and rarely done. Third, siting: religious orders that build new schools and clinics can screen locations for nearby industry before they break ground.

The usual caveats

These numbers measure proximity, not exposure. A church 800 m from a battery-recycling yard is not necessarily contaminated; it is a place worth testing, not a confirmed harm. The church data is also incomplete — OpenStreetMap maps urban buildings far better than rural ones, and it captures Christian places of worship but not mosques, temples, or gurdwaras. So these counts are lower bounds, and they say nothing about the many faith communities the data misses.

The headline holds regardless. The same crowded ground that puts a quarter-million schools near documented toxic sites puts tens of thousands of churches there too — and the people who lead those congregations are well placed to help do something about it.

Data and method: OpenStreetMap Christian places of worship (40 countries), cross-referenced against Pure Earth's Toxic Sites Identification Program and national contaminated-site registers. Faith-school counts from national school censuses. Underlying files in data/.
